# Ambassador Tasks — Plano

This document outlines all the ways volunteer ambassadors can contribute to building Plano into the definitive global catalogue of architecture. Tasks are grouped by area. Not all of these are supported in the product today; many represent the vision of what the ambassador programme can become as the platform grows.

---

## 1. Building Data — Completeness

The core mission of the catalogue is to have a record for every significant building in the world, with rich, accurate metadata. Ambassadors who know their city are best placed to fill these gaps.

### 1.1 Add missing buildings
Identify notable buildings in the ambassador's area that aren't in Plano yet and add them. This includes well-known landmarks, lesser-celebrated gems, modernist housing estates, civic buildings, religious buildings, and vernacular architecture worth preserving in record.

### 1.2 Complete missing metadata
Many buildings exist in the database as stubs — a name and a pin, nothing more. Ambassadors can research and fill in:
- Year of completion (and construction start, where notable)
- Functional category and typology (museum, housing block, bridge, etc.)
- Architectural style (Brutalism, Art Deco, High-Tech, etc.)
- Materials (concrete, brick, glass, steel, timber)
- Context (urban, waterfront, rural, etc.)
- Access details (can visitors enter? is booking required? is it free?)

### 1.3 Verify and correct inaccurate data
Cross-reference existing entries against authoritative sources (official building websites, architectural monographs, municipal heritage registers, Wikipedia) and flag or correct errors: wrong completion year, misattributed architect, incorrect location pin, wrong city.

### 1.4 Improve location precision
Many buildings are pinned with approximate coordinates. Ambassadors who live nearby can walk to the building and confirm an exact GPS coordinate, upgrading the pin from approximate to exact.

### 1.5 Update construction status
Buildings change state. A building under construction gets completed; a historic structure gets demolished. Ambassadors can flag these changes — Built → Lost, Under Construction → Built — to keep the catalogue current.

### 1.6 Add alternative names and local spellings
Many buildings are known by multiple names — a popular nickname, a previous name, a name in the local language. Ambassadors can add aliases that improve search discoverability for local users.

### 1.7 Document unbuilt and lost architecture
Unbuilt projects (competition entries, unrealised commissions) and demolished buildings are part of architectural history. Ambassadors with research skills can add these, flagged with the correct status, and contribute to a more complete historical record.

### 1.8 Add external references and links
Architecture enthusiasts often know the best resources: a short documentary, a heritage listing, a scholarly article, an official building page. Ambassadors can add these as links on building pages, helping other users go deeper.

### 1.9 Merge duplicate entries
The same building sometimes gets added twice under slightly different names. Ambassadors familiar with local buildings can spot these duplicates and initiate a merge to keep the database clean.

## 2. Building Data — Photography

Photography is the single most visible contribution an ambassador can make. A great photo transforms a stub into an inviting entry.

### 2.1 Photograph unphotographed buildings
Identify buildings in the chapter's area with no photos and go take them. A single good exterior shot is enough to make a building feel real.

### 2.2 Improve under-represented buildings
Some buildings have photos but they're poor quality — a blurry snapshot, a badly cropped image, an angle that doesn't show the building well. Ambassadors can add better photographs to raise the visual quality of the catalogue.

### 2.3 Capture interior shots
For buildings with public access — lobbies, atriums, public galleries — interior photography adds significant depth to the entry. Ambassadors who visit regularly are well placed to capture these.

### 2.4 Seasonal and time-of-day photography
The same building looks very different in summer versus winter, or at dusk versus noon. Ambassadors who return to a building across seasons can build a richer visual archive.

### 2.5 Photograph construction progress
For buildings currently under construction, periodic documentation photos create a valuable archive of the building's realisation — rare on any platform.

### 2.6 Flag inappropriate or low-quality images
Ambassadors can review the photo gallery for buildings in their area and flag images that are clearly incorrect (wrong building), misleading, or of very poor quality, helping maintain the visual standard of the catalogue.

## 3. Architect and Firm Data

An IMDB for architecture needs as much attention on the people as on the buildings. Ambassadors with professional networks or research skills can contribute here.

### 3.1 Identify unclaimed architect profiles
Many people in the Plano database haven't claimed their profile. Ambassadors — especially those with connections to local architecture schools, practices, or professional associations — can identify unclaimed profiles and reach out to those architects to invite them to claim their page.

### 3.2 Improve attribution accuracy
Some buildings are credited to the wrong person or firm, or credits are missing entirely. Ambassadors can research correct attributions through local archives, planning records, or professional contacts, and suggest corrections.

### 3.3 Add missing people and firms
Local architects and practices — especially those who are significant locally but not yet internationally known — may be entirely absent from the database. Ambassadors can add profiles and connect them to their buildings.

### 3.4 Verify biographical data
Birth and death years, nationality, education, and notable periods of practice all contribute to richer people profiles. Ambassadors with research skills can verify and improve this data.

### 3.5 Help firms connect to their buildings
Practices that haven't yet claimed their company profile on Plano can be identified and invited to do so. This is particularly valuable for regional firms with significant local work.

## 4. Translation and Localisation

Architecture is global. Plano should be accessible in every language.

### 4.1 Translate building names
Buildings often have an official local-language name that is different from the English name used in the catalogue. Ambassadors can add correct local-language names as alternative names, improving search discoverability for users in that country.

### 4.2 Write local-language descriptions
For the most significant buildings in a chapter's area, ambassadors with writing ability can contribute building descriptions in the local language, making the platform meaningfully accessible to users who don't read English.

### 4.3 Translate UI strings (future)
As Plano introduces localisation, ambassadors who are bilingual can help translate interface strings into their language, starting with the most-used surfaces.

### 4.4 Contextualise local architectural culture
Some architectural terms, movements, or periods are highly specific to a country or region and poorly understood outside it. Ambassadors can write notes or descriptions that give international users useful context.

## 5. Community Building and Outreach

Data quality improves when more knowledgeable people are contributing. Ambassadors are the most direct path to growing that community.

### 5.1 Welcome new members
When someone in the chapter's city joins Plano, a personal message or follow from a local ambassador goes a long way. Ambassadors can act as informal welcomers for their area.

### 5.2 Invite architects and firms
Every architect who claims their profile and adds their buildings becomes a contributor. Ambassadors who know local practices can make personal introductions to the platform — far more effective than cold email.

### 5.3 Outreach to architecture schools
Students and academics are among Plano's most engaged potential users. Ambassadors can reach out to local architecture schools, suggest Plano as a research or cataloguing tool, and run informal demonstrations.

### 5.4 Connect with heritage organisations
Preservation societies, heritage registers, and architectural archives often maintain building data that could improve Plano's catalogue. Ambassadors can build relationships with these organisations and explore data-sharing or collaborative enrichment.

### 5.5 Review chapter membership applications
Chapter leaders (president and ExCo) review applications from people who want to join the local chapter. Prompt and thoughtful review keeps the chapter healthy and welcoming.

### 5.6 Mentor new ambassadors
Experienced ambassadors can informally guide newly joined members on how to contribute effectively: how to photograph well, how to research attributions, how to use the Embassy dashboard.

## 6. Curation and Editorial

Beyond raw data, Plano is a discovery platform. Ambassadors who have strong local knowledge and writing ability can contribute at the editorial layer.

### 6.1 Build city and neighbourhood collections
Curated collections — "The 10 Essential Buildings of Porto", "Modernist Vienna: A Walking Route", "Emerging Architects in Seoul" — are one of the most shareable parts of Plano. Ambassadors who know a city well are ideally placed to build these.

### 6.2 Create architecture walk itineraries
A collection of buildings in a walkable neighbourhood, arranged as a self-guided tour, is genuinely useful to visitors. Ambassadors can design and document these routes using Plano's itinerary tools.

### 6.3 Write architect statements (with permission)
For buildings by local architects who are active community members, ambassadors may be able to help facilitate an official statement being added — or to write a brief contextual note where no statement exists.

### 6.4 Identify "hidden gems"
Not every great building is famous. Ambassadors who know their city can surface underappreciated buildings that deserve more attention, adding them to the database and writing notes that explain why they matter.

### 6.5 Document local architectural movements
Some cities have a distinct local architectural tradition or a period of exceptional production. Ambassadors can build thematic collections or add context that helps Plano users understand these movements.

## 7. Quality Assurance and Moderation

Ambassadors are a distributed quality layer for a database that will eventually contain millions of buildings.

### 7.1 Spot-check chapter area entries
A periodic review of buildings in the chapter's area — checking for obvious errors, outdated information, or low-quality entries — helps maintain baseline data quality without centralised editorial overhead.

### 7.2 Flag problematic content
Ambassadors can flag photos that are misattributed, reviews that are offensive, or building entries that appear to be spam or vandalism, escalating to the central team when needed.

### 7.3 Identify buildings that need urgent attention
Some buildings in the catalogue may have critically wrong information — the wrong location, a clearly incorrect date, a completely misidentified building. Ambassadors who spot these can escalate them for fast correction.

### 7.4 Cross-reference against heritage registers
Many countries maintain official heritage registers (listed buildings, protected monuments). Ambassadors can cross-reference Plano's catalogue against these registers to find gaps, add heritage status information, or verify data.

## 8. Events and Local Presence

Ambassadors are Plano's presence in the real world. Events are a high-leverage way to build community and content simultaneously.

### 8.1 Organise local architecture walks
A group walk with photographers and architecture enthusiasts is one of the fastest ways to add photos and reviews to under-documented buildings in a city. Ambassadors can organise these informally, sharing the date in their chapter.

### 8.2 Run photo days
A dedicated day where a group of ambassadors sets out to photograph a specific set of buildings with no photos — planned against the Embassy dashboard's task list — turns a backlog into a community event.

### 8.3 Host meet-ups for local architecture enthusiasts
An informal gathering at a notable building or local architecture school builds the community and creates a natural moment to invite attendees to join Plano.

### 8.4 Partner with architecture festivals and events
Architecture weeks, open house events, and biennales create concentrated moments where architectural interest is high. Ambassadors can represent Plano at these events, onboard new users, and capture content.

### 8.5 Document events in Plano
When a significant architectural event happens in the chapter's city — an opening, a competition result, a demolition — ambassadors can document it: photos, a review, a note on the relevant building page.

## 9. Chapter Leadership (President and ExCo)

These responsibilities are specific to ambassadors in leadership roles.

### 9.1 Coordinate chapter priorities
Each chapter has a finite pool of effort. Chapter leaders can review the Embassy dashboard, identify the highest-impact gaps (most-viewed buildings without photos, most-visited buildings with incomplete metadata), and share priorities with the chapter.

### 9.2 Review and approve membership applications
Leaders are responsible for ensuring new members are genuine and well-suited to the chapter. This includes reviewing motivation text, checking profile completeness, and making a prompt decision.

### 9.3 Handle location-change reviews
When an existing ambassador moves cities, their membership is flagged for review. Chapter leaders confirm whether the membership should continue, be transferred to another chapter, or be closed.

### 9.4 Communicate chapter news to the central team
Chapter leaders are the escalation path for issues the central team needs to know about: a significant building that's about to be demolished, a data problem that's widespread in the region, a partnership opportunity with a local institution.

### 9.5 Build and maintain chapter culture
A healthy chapter has engaged members who feel connected to each other and to the mission. Chapter leaders set the tone — recognising contributions, keeping communication active, and making the programme worth participating in.

## 10. Longer-term / Specialist Contributions

These tasks require specific skills or access and are not expected of every ambassador, but represent high-value contributions for those who can offer them.

### 10.1 Archival research
Access to local municipal archives, architectural firm archives, or national library collections can unlock data that doesn't exist anywhere online — floor plans, original drawings, construction records, historical photographs. Ambassadors with archival access can contribute this in the form of building descriptions, corrected dates, and historical photo uploads.

### 10.2 Academic collaboration
Ambassadors who are students or academics can propose Plano as a platform for architectural research projects — cataloguing a region, documenting a movement, surveying building types — generating high-quality structured contributions.

### 10.3 Translation of historical sources
Some of the best data about buildings exists only in the local language — planning documents, local architectural criticism, heritage assessments. Ambassadors with language skills can translate and distil this into building entries.

### 10.4 Drone photography (where permitted)
Aerial views of landmark buildings are rare in community-generated databases. Ambassadors who are licensed drone operators can contribute a category of photography that no casual user can.

### 10.5 3D documentation
For significant buildings, ambassadors with photogrammetry or LiDAR skills could contribute 3D scans or models — a long-term aspiration for the platform's archive.
